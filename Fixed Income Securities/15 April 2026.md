# Fixed Income (F.I.) Markets: Comprehensive Study Notes

Fixed Income Markets are where investors buy and sell debt securities (like bonds). In these markets, investors lend money to an entity (like a government or corporation) in exchange for regular interest payments and the return of their original investment when the bond matures.

## 1. Classification of Fixed Income Markets

Fixed income securities can be categorized in several ways to help investors understand the risks and features involved.

### A. Based on the Issuer

The #issuer is the entity borrowing the money.

- **Government & Government-Related Entities:** These are considered very safe.
    - _Examples:_ Treasury Bills (T-bills), Treasury Bonds (T-bonds).
    - _General Obligation (GO) Bonds:_ Backed by the credit and taxing power of the issuing jurisdiction.
    - _Revenue Bonds:_ Backed by the revenue generated by a specific project (e.g., a toll road).
    
- **Corporate Sector:** Companies issuing debt to fund their operations or expansion.
    
- **Structured Finance Sector:** Entities known as **Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs)** that issue securities backed by a pool of assets (more on this in the Securitization section).

### B. Based on Credit Quality
#Credit quality refers to the likelihood that the issuer will default (fail to pay back the debt).

- **Default Risk-Free:** Generally refers to government-backed securities or highly secure Money Market Funds (MMFs) handled by financial intermediaries.
- **Investment Grade:** High credit quality bonds with a low risk of default.
    - _Typical Investors:_ Bond funds, insurance firms, pension funds, and asset managers.
    
- **Non-Investment Grade (High-Yield / Junk Bonds):** Low credit quality bonds. Because they are riskier, they must offer higher interest rates to attract investors.
    
    - _Typical Investors:_ Hedge funds seeking higher returns.
    

### C. Based on Maturity

#Maturity is the length of time until the borrower must pay back the principal amount.

- **Money Market:** Short-term debt instruments that mature in **less than 1 year** (e.g., T-bills, Commercial Paper).

- **Capital Markets:** Long-term debt instruments that mature in **more than 1 year** (e.g., traditional Bonds).


### D. Based on Coupon

The "coupon" is the interest rate paid to the bondholder.

- **Fixed:** The interest rate stays the exact same for the entire life of the bond.
    
- **Floating:** The interest rate changes periodically based on a benchmark market rate.
    

### E. Based on Geography

- **Developed Markets:** Countries with mature economies (e.g., US, UK, Japan). They offer more mature markets and diversified risks.
    
- **Emerging Markets:** Developing economies. They offer higher potential returns but come with higher political and economic risks.
    

### F. Based on Currency Denomination

- **Foreign Currency:** Debt issued in a currency different from the issuer's home country.
    
- **Local Currency:** Debt issued in the issuer's domestic currency.

## 2. Market Structure: Primary vs. Secondary

### The Primary Market

This is where **new** securities are created and sold for the first time. The defining feature is that **money flows directly from the investors to the issuer.**

1. **Public Offering:** The securities are offered to the general investing public.
    
    - **Underwritten Offering:** An investment bank (underwriter) guarantees the sale of the bond issue. They buy the whole lot and take the risk of selling it to the public. They will provide a _Prospectus_ or _Offering Memorandum_(a legal document detailing the investment's facts and risks).
        
    - **Best Effort:** The investment bank acts as a broker and tries to sell as many bonds as possible, but does not guarantee that all will be sold.
        
    - **Auction:** Often used by governments (like US Treasuries). Involves competitive bids (investors specify the rate they want) and non-competitive bids (investors accept whatever rate is decided). Can be a single-price or multiple-price auction.
        
2. **Private Placement (Offering):** The securities are sold directly to a small group of select, usually institutional, investors rather than the general public.
    

### The Secondary Market

This is where **existing** (already issued) securities are traded among investors. The defining feature here is that the **issuer is not involved** and does not receive any new money from these trades.

1. **Organized Exchange:** A centralized, regulated marketplace where buyers and sellers meet to trade (e.g., the New York Stock Exchange).
    
2. **O.T.C. (Over-The-Counter):** A decentralized market where trading occurs directly between two parties (usually via dealer networks) rather than on a central exchange. Most bonds trade OTC.

## 3. Deep Dive into Sector-Specific Instruments

### Government-Related Debt

- **Instruments:** T-bills, T-bonds, General Obligation (GO) bonds, Revenue bonds.
    
- **Classification:** Can be External (foreign currency/investors) vs. Domestic (local currency/investors).
    
- **Driver:** The country's _Fiscal Policy_ (government spending and taxation) dictates the total quantum (amount) of debt the government needs to issue.
    

### Corporate Sector Debt

- **Commercial Paper:** Short-term, unsecured debt issued by corporations to cover short-term liabilities (like payroll).
    
- **Corporate Bonds:** Long-term debt issued by companies.
    
- **Repo Market (Repurchase Agreements):** A form of short-term borrowing where a party sells securities to investors and agrees to buy them back shortly after at a slightly higher price.
    

### Structured Financial Sector & Securitization

- **Instruments:** Asset-Backed Securities (ABS), Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDO), Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS).
    
- **The Securitization Process:** This is the process of taking illiquid assets (like a bunch of car loans or credit card debt) and packaging them together into a security that can be traded.
    
    1. **The Originator (Seller):** Has assets/collateral (like customer receivables/loans).
        
    2. **The SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle):** A separate legal entity created solely for this process. The Seller sells the receivables to the SPV.
        
    3. **The Investors:** The SPV packages these receivables into Asset-Backed Securities (A.B.S.) and sells them to Investors.
        
    4. **Cash Flow:** Investors give _Cash_ to the SPV to buy the A.B.S. The SPV passes that _Cash_ back to the Seller. As the original _Customers_ pay off their loans, that cash flows through the SPV back to the investors as returns.

## 4. Specific Types of Securities

### Participation Securities

These are bonds where the interest rate isn't entirely fixed.

- **Floaters (Floating Rate Notes):** The interest payment goes up or down with market rates.
    
    - _Formula:_ $Coupon = MRR + Spread$
        
    - _(MRR = Market Reference Rate, like SOFR or LIBOR; Spread = a fixed percentage added to the rate)._
        
- **Inverse Floaters:** The interest payment moves in the _opposite_ direction of market rates. If market rates go up, the bond's coupon goes down.
    
    - _Formula:_ $Coupon = Cap - (MRR \times Leverage Rate)$
        

### Capital Protected Security

- A financial instrument that guarantees the investor will at least get their original investment (the principal or "capital") back at maturity, regardless of how the underlying market performs, while offering some upside potential based on an underlying asset.
