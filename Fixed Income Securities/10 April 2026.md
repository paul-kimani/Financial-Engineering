**FIXED INCOME SECURITIES**

**Comprehensive Guide to Fixed Income Securities**

At its core, a fixed-income security is a **debt instrument**. It represents a contractual agreement between two primary parties:

- **The #Issuer:** The borrower who needs capital.
- **The #Investor:** The lender who provides the capital in exchange for regular interest payments and the return of principal.

To fully evaluate a fixed-income instrument, an investor must understand three fundamental pillars: **Bond Features**, **Legal/Regulatory & Tax Considerations**, and **Contingent Provisions**.

  

**1. Bond Features**

This section defines the fundamental characteristics of the bond, outlining who is borrowing, how they will pay interest, and when the debt will be repaid.

**A. The Issuer**

The entity issuing the bond determines its baseline credit risk. Issuers generally fall into these categories:

- **Government & Government-Related:**

- _Sovereign Governments:_ National governments issuing debt (e.g., Treasury Bonds, Eurobonds). They typically carry the lowest default risk in their local currency.
- _Non-Sovereign Governments:_ Local governments, states, or municipalities (e.g., county bonds).
- _Quasi-Government Entities:_ Agencies owned or sponsored by the government (e.g., parastatals like KenGen).
- _Supranational Entities:_ International organizations funded by multiple countries (e.g., The World Bank, IMF).

- **Corporations:** Private or public companies issuing debt to fund operations or expansion.
- **Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs):** Legal entities created for a specific financial transaction, commonly used in **Structured Products** and **Securitization** (e.g., pooling mortgages or auto loans into a tradable bond).

  

  

**B. Coupon Rate & Structures**

The coupon is the interest paid to the bondholder. Structures can vary significantly based on market conditions and issuer needs:

- **Fixed Coupon:** A static interest rate paid throughout the life of the bond.
- **Floating Coupon:** A variable rate tied to a benchmark.

- _Example:_ Central Bank Rate (CBR) + $x\%$, or a Market Reference Rate (MRR) + Spread. The spread compensates for the issuer's specific credit risk.

- **Indexed Coupon:** Payments are linked to an external metric, often inflation, to protect purchasing power. Types include:

- _Capital Indexed Bond:_ The principal amount adjusts with inflation; the fixed coupon rate is applied to the adjusted principal.
- _Zero-Coupon Indexed Bond:_ No periodic interest is paid; the inflation-adjusted principal is paid at maturity.
- _Coupon Rate Indexed:_ The coupon rate itself fluctuates with the index.
- _Annuity Indexed:_ Amortized payments (principal + interest) are adjusted for inflation.

- **Credit-Linked:** The coupon changes based on the issuer's credit rating (e.g., the coupon increases if the issuer is downgraded).
- **P.I.K. (Payment-in-Kind):** Allows the issuer to pay interest with additional bonds rather than cash. Often used by highly leveraged companies.
- **Deferred Coupon:** Interest payments are delayed for a specified initial period, typically used when a project takes time to generate cash flow.

  

  

**C. Par Value & Maturity**

- **Par Value:** The "paper" or face value of the bond. This is the principal amount that will be returned to the investor at maturity.
- **Maturity:** The timeline of the debt, broadly categorized into Short-term vs. Long-term.
- **Principal & Repayment Structures:**

- _Bullet Structure:_ The entire principal is paid at once at maturity.
- _Amortized Structure:_ The principal is paid down gradually over the life of the bond alongside interest.
- _Sinking Fund Arrangement:_ The issuer is required to retire a portion of the bond issue periodically before maturity, reducing the risk of a massive single payout at the end.
- _Currency Denomination:_ Identifies the currency in which payments are made (vital for assessing foreign exchange risk).

  

  

**2. Legal, Regulatory & Tax Considerations**

This pillar covers the rules governing the bond and how the resulting income is treated by authorities.

**A. The Bond Indenture (Legal)**

The indenture is the binding master legal contract between the issuer and the bondholders. It explicitly details:

- **Legal Identity of the Issuer:** Exactly which entity is legally responsible for the debt.
- **Source of Repayment Proceeds:** Where the cash flow to pay investors will come from (e.g., general taxation for sovereigns, project revenues for infrastructure bonds).
- **Collateral:** Any specific assets pledged to secure the debt in case of default.
- **Covenants:** Legally binding rules the issuer must follow to protect bondholders.

- _Affirmative Covenants (The Do's):_ Actions the issuer _must_ take. Examples: Sticking to stated business policies, paying taxes on time, and complying with all local laws.
- _Negative Covenants (The Don'ts):_ Actions the issuer _cannot_ take. Examples: Taking on additional senior debt, engaging in speculative investments, or issuing new debt unless it is subordinated to the current bond.

  

  

**B. Taxation**

Understanding the tax drag is critical for calculating the true yield of a bond. As noted in your local market context:

- **Tax on Interest:** The regular coupon payments are typically subject to a Withholding Tax (e.g., the 15% standard rate in Kenya).
- **Tax on Capital Gains (C.G.T):** If the bond is sold in the secondary market at a price higher than its purchase price, the profit may be subject to Capital Gains Tax (e.g., 15%). _Note: Tax codes frequently exempt certain government bonds (like infrastructure bonds) from these taxes to incentivize investment._

  

  

  

**3. Contingent Provisions (Embedded Options)**

Contingent provisions are embedded options that give either the issuer or the investor the right to take a specific action under certain conditions.

- **Call Provisions (Callable Bond):**

- _Formula:_ Option-Free Bond + Call Option (held by Issuer)
- _Mechanism:_ The issuer has the right to redeem the bond before maturity. They will typically do this if market interest rates drop, allowing them to refinance the debt at a cheaper rate.
- _Exercise Styles:_ **American** (callable at any time after the lockout period), **European** (callable only on one specific date), or **Bermudan** (callable on specific dates, usually coupon payment dates).

- **Put Provisions (Putable Bond):**

- _Formula:_ Option-Free Bond + Put Option (held by Investor)
- _Mechanism:_ The investor has the right to sell the bond back to the issuer at a predetermined price before maturity. Investors use this if market interest rates rise, allowing them to reinvest their capital at higher prevailing rates.

- **Conversion Provisions (Convertible Bond):**

- _Formula:_ Option-Free Bond + Call Option on Issuer's Shares
- _Mechanism:_ Gives the investor the right to convert the bond into a specified number of common shares of the issuing company. This allows debt investors to participate in the equity upside if the company performs exceptionally well.
