## **1. Core Concept: Bond Valuation**

At its heart, the value of a bond is simply the **Present Value (PV)** of all its future cash flows (coupons and the final principal repayment), discounted at the market interest rate.

### **The Valuation Formula**

If a bond pays a constant coupon $C$ and a principal $P$ at maturity, the price is:

$$PV = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{C}{(1+r)^t} + \frac{P}{(1+r)^n}$$

Where:

- **$C$**: Coupon payment.
- **$r$**: Market Discount Rate (Yield).
- **$n$**: Number of periods to maturity.
- **$P$**: Par value (Face value).

### **Valuation using Spot Rates**

Your notes correctly show that we can use different discount rates for each specific time period ($z_1, z_2, \dots, z_n$). These are called **Spot Rates**.

$$PV = \frac{C}{(1+z_1)^1} + \frac{C}{(1+z_2)^2} + \dots + \frac{C+P}{(1+z_n)^n}$$

## **2. Bond Pricing Relationships**

The relationship between the **Coupon Rate ($C$)** and the **Market Yield ($r$)** determines if a bond is expensive or cheap:

|**Condition**|**Bond Status**|**Definition**|
|---|---|---|
|**$C > r$**|**Premium**|The bond pays more than the market; it sells for _more_ than its face value.|
|**$C = r$**|**Par**|The bond pays exactly the market rate; it sells _at_ face value.|
|**$C < r$**|**Discount**|The bond pays less than the market; it sells for _less_ than its face value.|

---

## **3. Yield to Maturity (YTM) Assumptions**

Your notes highlight three critical assumptions for YTM to be an accurate measure of return:

1. **No Default:** The issuer makes all payments on time (Zero Credit Risk).
2. **Hold to Maturity:** The investor doesn't sell the bond before it ends (Zero Market Price Risk).
3. **Reinvestment at YTM:** All coupon payments are reinvested at the same rate as the YTM (Zero Reinvestment Risk).

## **4. Clean Price vs. Dirty Price**

This is where it gets practical for trading. In the real world, bonds are often traded between coupon dates.

- **Accrued Interest (A.I.):** The portion of the next coupon that the seller has "earned" by holding the bond since the last payment.
- **Full Price (Dirty Price):** What the buyer actually pays. It includes the accrued interest.
- **Flat Price (Clean Price):** The quoted price in the market. It excludes accrued interest to prevent "price jumps" on payment dates.

### **The Math**

$$\text{Full Price} = \text{Clean Price} + \text{Accrued Interest}$$

$$\text{Accrued Interest (A.I.)} = \frac{t}{T} \times \text{Coupon}$$

- $t$ = days from the last coupon to the settlement date. 
- $T$ = total days in the coupon period.

## **5. Example Walkthrough (From your Notes)**

**Scenario:**

- **Par Value (P.V.):** 100
- **Coupon ($C$):** $6\%$ (Assume $6$ annually, or $3$ semi-annually).
- **Settlement Date:** 15 April 2021
- **Coupon Dates:** 30 June & 31 December
- **YTM:** $4\%$

**To solve for the Bond Price on 15 April 2021:**

1. **Find the Next Cash Flow:** The next coupon of $3$ occurs on 30 June.
2. **Calculate $t$:** Days from 31 Dec (last coupon) to 15 April.
3. **Calculate $T$:** Days from 31 Dec to 30 June.
4. **Discount the Cash Flows:** Discount all remaining coupons and the principal back to the settlement date using the $4\%$ YTM.
5. **Identify Price:** The result of the PV calculation is the **Dirty Price**. Subtract the **A.I.** to find the **Clean Price**.

Since $C (6\%) > \text{YTM } (4\%)$, you should expect the price to be at a **Premium** (higher than 100).

### **A Quick Note on Conventions**

Your notes mention **30/360** vs **Actual/Actual**.

- **30/360:** Assumes every month has 30 days (common for corporate bonds).
- **Actual/Actual:** Uses the real number of days (common for Government/Treasury bonds).
