## **Comprehensive Notes: Introduction to Financial Economics**

### **1. The Domain of Financial Economics and Finance**

To understand the theory of finance, we first need to look at its broader context: **Financial Economics**.

- **Definition of Financial Economics:** A field of study that sits directly at the intersection of finance and economics. It analyzes the relationship between various financial variables, such as asset prices, interest rates, and share values.

- **The Role of Finance:** Finance is a branch of economics (a social science). While economics generally studies how people allocate scarce resources among competing objectives, finance specifically studies how people choose between **uncertain future values**.


**The Three Pillars of Financial Decisions:**

Any economic problem studied in finance involves three critical alternatives:

1. **Time:** When do you invest, and how long do you wait for a return? (e.g., tying up your money for 10 years in a retirement fund).

2. **Money:** The capital being allocated (e.g., investing $10,000).

3. **Uncertainty (Risk):** The likelihood that the future outcome will differ from what you expect (e.g., the risk that a stock you buy today drops in value tomorrow).

### **2. The Research Cycle in Financial Economics**

Financial economics relies on an interlocking cycle of scientific and applied research to develop and test its theories. This cycle consists of three continuous phases:

- **Theoretical Phase:**
    
    - **Assumptions:** Foundational beliefs about how the market works (e.g., "investors are rational").
        
    - **Model:** The creation of a mathematical framework based on those assumptions.
        
    - **Predictions:** What the model says _should_ happen in the real world.
        
- **Empirical Phase:**
    
    - **Prices/Hypotheses:** Observing actual market data (like stock prices) or forming testable hypotheses.
        
    - **Tests:** Running statistical analyses to see if the real-world data aligns with the theory.
        
    - **Results:** The outcome of those tests, which either validate or challenge the theoretical predictions.
        
- **Practical Phase:**
    
    - **Applications:** Using the validated models in the real world (e.g., a hedge fund using a pricing model to buy undervalued stocks).
        
    - **Conclusions/Questions:** Observations from real-world application that feed back into the Theoretical phase to refine assumptions and start the cycle over.

### **3. Modelling Financial Markets**

A major focus of financial economics is the creation of market models.

- **Definition of a Model:** The activity of generating representative mathematical equation(s) that elaborate on a specific economic activity. These models are based on economic theory and primarily aim to **predict security prices and their returns.**
    
- **Market Efficiency:** The choice and accuracy of any financial model heavily depend on the _efficiency of the market_.
    
    - _Definition:_ An efficient market is one where current prices reflect all available information.
        
    - _Example:_ If a company suddenly announces a breakthrough invention, an _efficient_ market will instantly adjust the company's stock price upward. If the market is inefficient, the price might take days to adjust, throwing off predictive models.
        
- **Market Equilibrium:** Models often assume a market is in balance (equilibrium). When a market is _out of equilibrium_ (experiencing panic selling, bubbles, or liquidity crises), the resulting parameters of your model may deviate significantly from what they would be in a normal, balanced market.

### **4. The Foundation: Expected Utility Theory**

The bedrock of these financial models is the **Expected Utility Theory**. Financial models start with the basic assumption that investors aim to maximize their expected utility.

- **Definition of Expected Utility Theory:** When an individual makes an investment choice, they should choose the action that results in the highest _expected utility_ (satisfaction or personal value), **not** necessarily the action that yields the highest expected _wealth_ (pure monetary value).
    
- **Utility vs. Wealth:** This theory is crucial because it highlights that the _utility_ of money is not the same as the _total value_ of money. It focuses on the trade-offs investors are willing to make based on their personal risk tolerance.
    

> **Example of Expected Utility:** > Imagine you are offered a choice: a 100% guarantee of receiving $50,000, or a 50% chance of winning $100,000 (and a 50% chance of getting nothing).
> 
> - The _expected wealth_ of both choices is exactly the same ($50,000).
>     
> - However, for most people, the _utility_ (peace of mind, ability to pay off debt immediately) of a guaranteed $50,000 is much higher than the anxiety of potentially getting nothing. Therefore, they choose the guaranteed money to maximize their _utility_, even if a purely mathematical wealth model says both options are equal.
