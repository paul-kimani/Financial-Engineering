## **1. The Core Objective of Financial Decision-Making**

The central theme of this theory is understanding how individuals (and firms) make choices when faced with **uncertain payoffs over multiple time periods**.

- **The Goal:** To mathematically model consumption and investment decisions, specifically examining how interest rates influence whether we spend today or save for tomorrow.
    

---

## **2. Mapping Preferences: Indifference Curves & MRS**

To model an investor's preferences, we use **Indifference Curves**. An indifference curve is a graph showing different bundles of current consumption ($C_0$) and future consumption ($C_1$) that give the investor the exact same level of total utility (satisfaction).

### **The Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)**

If you pick a specific point on an indifference curve (like Point B in your slides) and draw a straight tangent line against it, the slope of that line is the **Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)**.

- **Definition:** The MRS is the subjective rate at which an individual is willing to trade current consumption for future consumption _without changing their overall total utility_.
    
- **The Concept:** It represents the investor's personal "exchange rate" for time. It answers the question: _"How many extra units of tomorrow-money must I receive to willingly give up one unit of today-money, and still feel equally happy?"_
    
- **Changing Slopes:** Because the curve is convex, the MRS changes depending on where you are. If you have very little current wealth ($C_0$), your rate of time preference is high—you will demand a lot of future compensation to give up what little you have today.
    

_(Note: Don't confuse **MRS** with the **MRT** from the previous unit. MRT is the objective market trade-off; MRS is the subjective personal trade-off)._

## **3. The Foundation of Rationality: Axioms of Choice**

For these mathematical models to work, we have to assume the investor is rational. We define "rational behavior" using three **Axioms of Choice**. An axiom is an underlying assumption accepted as true without needing proof. They are the minimum conditions required to build our economic models.

### **Axiom 1: Completeness (or Comparability)**

An investor can always look at any two alternatives ($a_1$ and $a_2$) and make up their mind. There is no such thing as "I don't know." The entire space of choice can be mapped.

You must be able to state one of three things:

1. $a_1$ is preferred to $a_2$ ($a_1 \succ a_2$)
    
2. $a_2$ is preferred to $a_1$ ($a_2 \succ a_1$)
    
3. You are indifferent between the two ($a_1 \sim a_2$)
    

### **Axiom 2: Non-Satiation (Greed)**

"More is always better." Holding all other things equal, an individual will always prefer a bundle that gives them more of an asset over one that gives them less.

- **Graphical Impact:** This is why indifference curves further up and to the right on a graph represent higher utility ($I_3 > I_2 > I_1$). Investors always desire more current _and_ more future consumption.
    

### **Axiom 3: Transitivity (Consistency)**

An investor's preferences must be logically consistent.

- If you prefer A to B ($A \succ B$), and you prefer B to C ($B \succ C$), then logic dictates you **must** prefer A to C ($A \succ C$).
- The same applies to indifference: If $A \sim B$ and $B \sim C$, then $A \sim C$.


**The Rule of Non-Intersection:**

Axiom 3 strictly dictates that **indifference curves can never intersect**.

Look at your final slide: If Curve 1 and Curve 2 cross at point B, it creates a logical paradox. The investor would simultaneously be indifferent between A and B, indifferent between B and C, but clearly prefer A over C (because A has more goods, per the Non-Satiation axiom). This breaks the laws of rational choice!
