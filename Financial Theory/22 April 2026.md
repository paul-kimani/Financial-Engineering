## **1. The Foundation: The Consumption Opportunity Set**

Before looking at time, we must understand basic consumption choices.

- **Opportunity Set:** The complete set of all possible combinations (bundles) of goods you can afford, given your current income and the market prices of those goods.
- **Budget Constraint:** The boundary of your opportunity set. It represents the exact limit of what you can afford if you spend all your resources. It illustrates the real-world **trade-off**—to get more of Good A, you must give up some of Good B.
- **Marginal Rate of Transformation (MRT):** The slope of the opportunity set. It defines the exact rate at which you must sacrifice one good to "transform" your purchasing power into another good.

## **2. Adding Time: Current vs. Future Consumption**

Instead of choosing between two physical goods (like apples and oranges), corporate finance and economics treat time periods as the "goods."

- **Current Consumption ($x_0$):** The money you spend today.    
- **Future Consumption ($x_1$):** The money you save/invest today so you can spend it tomorrow.

If you save a portion of your wealth today, you can transform it into _more_ than one unit of consumption tomorrow by investing it.

- **The Capital Market Rate ($r$):** If you invest (or lend) in the financial markets, the terms of trade are defined by the interest rate $r$. For every KES 1 you defer today, you get $1(1 + r)$ in the future.

## **3. The Real Investment Decision**

Now, remove the financial markets (no lending or borrowing at a flat interest rate $r$). Imagine you can only grow your wealth by undertaking **real investments**—like buying machinery, building infrastructure, or funding a startup.

### **The Mechanics of the Concave Frontier**

Your slides show a graph with a curved, concave line (Frontier $F$) connecting current consumption on the x-axis to future consumption on the y-axis. Here is how to read it:

- **Initial Wealth ($W_0$):** Your starting point on the far right of the x-axis. If you invest nothing, you consume all your wealth today ($x_0 = W_0$) and have nothing tomorrow.
- **Making an Investment:** To get future wealth, you must sacrifice some current consumption. The amount you invest is mathematically: $W_0 - x_0$. By moving left on the x-axis (reducing current consumption), you move up the y-axis (gaining future consumption).
- **The Concave Shape (Diminishing Returns):** Unlike a flat bank interest rate, real investments have **diminishing returns to scale**. Your first investment (e.g., buying your first machine) yields massive returns. But as you keep investing (buying a 10th machine), the extra return generated by each new machine gets smaller.

---

## **4. The Math: MRT in Real Investments**

In real investments, the Marginal Rate of Transformation (MRT) is constantly changing because the curve is bending.

- **The Formula:** The MRT is the absolute value of the slope of the frontier at any given point:
    $$MRT = - \frac{dx_1}{dx_0}$$
    
    _(This simply means: the change in future consumption divided by the change in current consumption)._
- **The Rate of Return ($i$):** We can express the MRT as $(1 + i)$, where **$i$** is the **real rate of return** on the _very last_ shilling you invested (the marginal investment).
    
    $$MRT = 1 + i$$
    
### **The Golden Rule of the Frontier:**

Because of diminishing returns, as the level of real investment _increases_ (as you move further left on the x-axis and higher on the y-axis), the marginal rate of return ($i$) _decreases_. Therefore, the **MRT declines as real investment increases**, causing the slope to flatten out at the top.
