
## **1. The Two Pillars of Financial Modeling**

When modeling financial markets, quants generally use two main approaches: **Economic Models** and **Statistical Models**.

### **A. Economic Models (The "Why")**

Economic models are simplified versions of reality built from the ground up using **economic theory**.

- **Core Assumption:** They start with a fundamental belief about human behavior. In finance, the bedrock assumption is usually that **investors aim to maximize their expected utility** (they want the most reward for the least risk).
    
- **Purpose:** They generate mathematical equations that describe _why_ investors behave the way they do and how markets _should_ theoretically operate.
    

**Example: The CAPM Model**

The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) is a classic economic model.

$$E(R_i) = R_f + \beta_i\{E(R_m) - R_f\}$$

- _Why is it an economic model?_ It is founded entirely on **Mean-Variance Portfolio Theory**. It assumes investors only care about two things: the mean (expected return) and the variance (risk), and act rationally to maximize their utility based on these two metrics.
    

### **B. Statistical Models (The "How")**

Statistical models ignore economic theory and focus entirely on **data**.

- **Calibration:** The model's parameters are chosen specifically to fit historical data as closely as possible.
    
- **Pros & Cons:** Because they are fitted to actual data, they often feel more realistic and explain _how_ a phenomenon occurs. However, they **do not explain why**. Furthermore, if the historical data is flawed or anomalous, the model's credibility breaks down.
    

**Example: Black-Scholes Option Pricing**

The Black-Scholes formula for a call option ($C$) is a statistical model.

$$C = N(d_1)S_t - N(d_2)Ke^{-rt}$$

- _Why is it a statistical model?_ It assumes the price of the underlying asset follows a **Geometric Brownian Motion** (a specific type of random walk). This assumption isn't derived from human behavior or economic theory; it is chosen simply because it mathematically matches observed market data.
    

### **C. Time Series Models (The Hybrid)**

Time series models analyze data collected over time (e.g., daily stock prices). Depending on how you constrain the formula, it can lean economic or statistical:

- **As an Economic Model:** The formula's parameters are restricted to make sure they don't violate economic theory.
    
- **As a Statistical Model:** The formula (like your slide's example: $r_t = r_{t-1} + \delta^2 * r_{t-2}$) relies purely on finding the best fit for past historical data.

## **2. What Makes a "Good" Model?**

According to your slides, a successful financial model must balance three criteria:

1. **Data Fit:** It provides a reasonable fit to historical data, giving us confidence it describes the actual process.
    
2. **Theoretical Consistency:** It aligns with economic theory so we actually understand _why_ the market is behaving that way.
    
3. **Parsimony:** It is reasonably simple to develop and use. A "parsimonious" model is one that achieves its goal with the fewest and simplest assumptions possible. Overly complex models break easily.

## **3. The Financial Modeler's Manifesto**

Your slides include a crucial warning for quants. Financial theory reduces complex, chaotic markets into "universal laws" and often reduces risk to a single variable (like volatility). This ignores the reality that markets are full of irrational, biased people.

**The Modeler's Oath (Key Takeaways):**

- _Models are not reality:_ The world doesn't perfectly satisfy mathematical equations.
    
- _Elegance vs. Reality:_ Never sacrifice reality just to make the math look elegant or simple.
    
- _Transparency:_ Never give false comfort about a model's accuracy. Always state your assumptions and oversights explicitly.

## **4. Tying it Back: The Consumption/Savings Decision**

The slides return to the foundational economic model we looked at previously: the two-period model of current vs. future consumption.

If you have an initial wealth of $W_0$, you must make a single decision:

- **Current Consumption ($C_0$):** What you spend today.
    
- **Savings/Investment ($W_0 - C_0$):** What you defer to support your future consumption.
    

### **How do we solve this choice problem?**

To figure out exactly how much an investor _should_ consume vs. save, we need two pieces of the puzzle:

1. **The Opportunity Set (The "Can-Do"):** The feasible trade-offs available in the market (which we covered with the concave real investment frontier).
    
2. **Indifference Curves (The "Want-To"):** A map of the investor's personal preferences. An indifference curve is a line showing all the different combinations of current and future consumption that give the investor the exact same level of total satisfaction (utility). The investor is "indifferent" to any point on that specific curve.
    

The optimal decision is found exactly where the investor's highest possible indifference curve touches their opportunity set!
