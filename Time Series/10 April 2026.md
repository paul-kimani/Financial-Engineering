
**Fundamentals of Time Series Analysis**

**1. Introduction and Definition**

A **Time Series**, denoted mathematically as $\{X_t\}$ for $t \ge 0$, is a sequence of observations recorded sequentially over time.

- **$X$** represents the variable being measured.
- **$t$** represents the time index, drawn from a set $T$ (usually starting from $0$ to $n$).

The defining characteristic of a time series is that **the order of the data matters**. Unlike cross-sectional data (where observations are independent of one another), time series data points are inherently linked to their chronological sequence.

**Common Examples:**

- **Financial Markets:** Daily closing prices of a specific equity (e.g., Safaricom shares on the NSE), high-frequency tick data, or weekly trading volumes.
- **Macroeconomics:** The annual GDP of a country, or monthly inflation rates.
- **Physical Sciences:** Hourly temperature readings or continuous heart rate monitor data.
- **Business Operations:** Weekly sales data from a supermarket.

  

**2. Classification of Time Series**

Understanding the "type" of time series you are dealing with dictates the mathematical models you will use to analyze it. Your notes effectively break these down into four main categories:

**A. By the Time Domain (When are observations made?)**

- **Continuous Time Series:** Observations are measured continuously at every single instant in time.

- _Example:_ An electrocardiogram (ECG) monitoring a heartbeat, or a continuous temperature gauge in a chemical reactor.

- **Discrete Time Series:** Observations are recorded only at specific, usually equally spaced, time intervals.

- _Example:_ Daily stock market closing prices. Even though the price of a stock fluctuates continuously throughout the trading day, recording only the "closing price" turns it into a discrete time series.

**B. By Predictability (How random is the data?)**

- **Deterministic Time Series:** The future values can be predicted exactly from past observations using a specific mathematical formula. There is no randomness involved. If you know the past, you know the future with 100% certainty.

- _Example:_ A fixed-rate bank deposit growing with compound interest.

- **Stochastic Time Series:** The future is only _partly_ determined by past events. Exact predictions are impossible due to inherent randomness or "noise." Instead of predicting a single exact value, we model the _probability distribution_ of future values.

- _Context:_ **Almost all financial market data is stochastic.** You cannot predict tomorrow's exact stock price, but you can use stochastic modeling to estimate the probability that it will fall within a certain range.

**C. By Dimensionality (How many variables are tracked?)**

- **Univariate Time Series:** A single variable observed over time.

- _Example:_ Tracking _only_ the daily closing price of a stock.

- **Multivariate Time Series:** Multiple variables observed simultaneously over time.

- _Example:_ An algorithmic trading model that simultaneously tracks a stock's closing price, its daily trading volume, and the broader market index return. These variables often interact with and influence each other.

**D. By Statistical Properties (Does the behavior change over time?)**

This is arguably the most critical concept when preparing data for quantitative modeling.

- **Stationary Time Series:** A time series whose statistical properties—specifically its **mean** (average) and **variance**(volatility)—remain constant over time. It doesn't trend upwards or downwards, and its fluctuations remain roughly the same size.
- **Non-Stationary Time Series:** A time series where the statistical properties change over time. It might have a clear upward trend, or its volatility might cluster (periods of high variance followed by periods of low variance).

**The Modeling Challenge:**

Your notes mention, _"Times series models takes care non stationarity."_ To expand on this: Most standard time series forecasting models (like ARMA) absolutely require the data to be **stationary** to work correctly.

Because financial asset prices (like the absolute price of a stock) are almost always non-stationary, quantitative analysts must transform the data to make it stationary before feeding it into an algorithm. The most common way to do this is by calculating the **logarithmic returns** or taking the **first difference** ($X_t - X_{t-1}$) of the prices, shifting the focus from the _absolute price_ to the _rate of change_.
