### Deriving Itô's Lemma

Let $X_t$ be a stochastic process governed by the general Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE):

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t, t)dt + \sigma(X_t, t)dW_t$$
[[Stochastics/10 April 2026|10 April 2026]]
[[Proof of Ito's Lemma]]

If we define a function $f(X_t, t)$ that is twice continuously differentiable in $X_t$ and once in $t$, Itô's Lemma states how to find its differential $df(X_t, t)$.

To derive this, we use the Taylor Series Expansion. Recall the Taylor series for a single variable generated by the power series:

$$a_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}$$

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n (x-a)^n$$

$$f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2!}(x-a)^2 + \dots$$

Expanding this to a function of two variables $f(X, t)$, the change in $f$, denoted as $\Delta f$, is:

$$\Delta f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}\Delta t + \frac{\partial f}{\partial X}\Delta X + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X^2}(\Delta X)^2 + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2}(\Delta t)^2 + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X \partial t}\Delta X \Delta t + \dots$$

As $\Delta t \to 0$, we transition to differentials. Quantities of higher order (like $(\Delta t)^2$ or $\Delta X \Delta t$) become infinitesimally small and approach zero faster than the leading terms, so they drop out.

Analyzing the $(\Delta X)^2$ term, recall that our simpler SDE representation is:

$$\Delta X = \mu_t dt + \sigma_t dW_t$$

Squaring this gives:

$$(\Delta X)^2 = (\mu_t dt + \sigma_t dW_t)^2$$

$$(\Delta X)^2 = \mu_t^2 (dt)^2 + 2\mu_t\sigma_t (dt)(dW_t) + \sigma_t^2(dW_t)^2$$

Using the rules of "Box Calculus" (detailed below), we know that $(dt)^2 = 0$, $(dt)(dW_t) = 0$, and $(dW_t)^2 = dt$.

Therefore, the equation simplifies strictly to:

$$(dX_t)^2 = \sigma^2 dt$$

Substituting this back into our Taylor series expansion for $df$:

$$df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial X}dX_t + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X^2}(dX_t)^2$$

$$df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial X}[\mu dt + \sigma dW_t] + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X^2}(\sigma^2 dt)$$

Grouping the $dt$ and $dW_t$ terms gives the standard form of **Itô's Lemma**:

$$df = \left\{ \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \mu \frac{\partial f}{\partial X} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X^2} \right\} dt + \sigma \frac{\partial f}{\partial X} dW_t$$

### General Itô's Lemma (Multi-Dimensional)

For a function of time and two stochastic variables $f(X_1, X_2, t)$, the differential expands to include cross-derivative terms:

$$df(X_1, X_2) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial X_1}dX_1 + \frac{\partial f}{\partial X_2}dX_2 + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X_1^2}(dX_1)^2 + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X_2^2}(dX_2)^2 + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X_1 \partial X_2}(dX_1)(dX_2)$$

### The Box Calculus (Multiplication Table for Infinitesimal Increments)

In stochastic calculus, the quantity $dt$ represents an infinitesimal time interval—one so small that quantities of higher order (e.g., $(dt)^2, (dt)^3$) can be ignored or considered to be $0$.

### Examples of Diffusion Processes

Below are standard diffusion processes.

**1. Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)**

- **SDE:** $dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t$

- **Solution:** $S_t = S_0 \exp\left\{ \left(\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t \right\}$


**2. Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (O-U) Process**

- **SDE:** $dX_t = -\theta X_t dt + \sigma dW_t$ (where $\theta > 0$)

    - _Alternative Mean-Reverting Form:_ $dX_t = \theta(\mu - X_t)dt + \sigma dW_t$

- **Solution:** $X_t = X_0 e^{-\theta t} + \mu(1 - e^{-\theta t}) + \sigma \int_0^t e^{-\theta(t-s)} dW_s$


**3. Vasicek Model** 

The Vasicek model is essentially an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process used to model interest rates. It assumes the interest rate $r_t$ mean-reverts to a long-term level $b$ at a speed $a$.

- **SDE:** $dr_t = a(b - r_t)dt + \sigma dW_t$

- **Solution:** $r_t = r_0 e^{-at} + b(1 - e^{-at}) + \sigma \int_0^t e^{-a(t-s)} dW_s$

	- _Note:_ A well-known drawback of the Vasicek model is that the normal distribution of the diffusion term theoretically allows interest rates to become negative.


**4. Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model** _(Completed)_

It is an extension of the Vasicek model designed to enforce non-negative constraints on interest rates by making the volatility proportional to the square root of the rate itself.

- **SDE:** $dr_t = a(b - r_t)dt + \sigma \sqrt{r_t} dW_t$
    
- **Solution:** $r_t = r_0 e^{-at} + b(1 - e^{-at}) + \sigma \int_0^t e^{-a(t-s)} \sqrt{r_s} dW_s$
    
    - _Note:_ Unlike the Vasicek model, CIR does not have a simple exact closed-form solution based on a normal distribution. Because of the square root diffusion term, the future interest rate $r_t$ follows a _non-central chi-squared distribution_. As long as the Feller condition ($2ab \geq \sigma^2$) is met, the rate $r_t$ will remain strictly positive.
