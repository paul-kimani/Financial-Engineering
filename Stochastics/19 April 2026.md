### 1. Key Terminology and Definitions

To build a strong foundation for the mathematical steps in the notes, here are the definitions of the core concepts being applied:

- **Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE):**ô A differential equation in which one or more of the terms is a stochastic (random) process, resulting in a solution that is also a stochastic process.
    
- **Wiener Process / Brownian Motion ($w_t$):**ô A continuous-time random process with independent, normally distributed increments. It is the standard mathematical model for random noise or market fluctuations.
    
- **Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM):**ô A continuous-time stochastic process in which the logarithm of the randomly varying quantity follows a Brownian motion with drift. It is the standard model used for financial assets like share prices because it ensures prices remain positive.
    
- **Drift ($\mu$):**ô The deterministic, predictable trend or average rate of growth of the asset over time.
    
- **Volatility ($\sigma$):**ô The stochastic component representing the amplitude of the random fluctuations (risk) in the asset's path.
    
- **ItûÇ's Lemma:**ô In stochastic calculus, ItûÇ's Lemma serves as the equivalent of the chain rule in standard calculus. It is used to find the differential of a time-dependent function of a stochastic process.
    

---

### 2. Core Foundations & Multiplication Rules

The SDE of a share price follows a Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM). The general equation is:

$$dS_{t}=\mu S_{t}dt+\sigma S_{t}dw_{t}$$

To apply ItûÇ's Lemma to a functionô $f(t, S_t)$, the general formula is:

$$df(t,S_{t})=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial f}{\partial S_{t}}dS_{t}+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^{2}f}{\partial S_{t}^{2}}(dS_{t})^{2}$$

A critical part of calculating theô $(dS_t)^2$ô term relies on the multiplication rules for ItûÇ calculus:

- $(dt)^2 = 0$
    
- $dw_t \cdot dt = 0$
    
- $(dw_t)^2 = dt$
    

Because of these rules, when you expandô $(dS_t)^2 = (\mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dw_t)^2$ô , the terms containingô $(dt)^2$andô $dt \cdot dw_t$ô evaluate to zero. Thus, the expansion simplifies strictly to:

$$(dS_{t})^{2}=\sigma^{2}S_{t}^{2}dt$$

### 3. Example 1: SDE of a Forward Contract

**The Problem:**ô Find the SDE of a forward contract at timeô $t$ô using ItûÇ's Lemma. The value of the forward contract is defined as:

$$F_{t}=S_{t}e^{r(T-t)}$$

**Step 1: Calculate the Partial Derivatives**

- **With respect to time ($t$):**
    
    $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} = S_t e^{r(T-t)} \cdot (-r) = -rS_{t}e^{r(T-t)}$$
    
- **With respect to the underlying asset ($S_t$):**
    
    $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial S_{t}} = e^{r(T-t)} \cdot 1 = e^{r(T-t)}$$
    
- **Second derivative with respect to ($S_t$):**
    
    $$\frac{\partial^{2}f}{\partial S_{t}^{2}} = 0$$
    

**Step 2: Substitute into ItûÇ's Lemma**ô Substitute the calculated derivatives into the main lemma:

$$df(t,S_{t})=-rS_{t}e^{r(T-t)}dt+e^{r(T-t)}dS_{t}+0$$

**Step 3: Substitute the GBM Equation**ô Replaceô $dS_t$ô with the GBM formulaô $(\mu S_{t}dt+\sigma S_{t}dw_{t})$:

$$df(t,S_{t})=-rS_{t}e^{r(T-t)}dt+e^{r(T-t)}\{\mu S_{t}dt+\sigma S_{t}dw_{t}\}$$

$$df(t,S_{t})=-rS_{t}e^{r(T-t)}dt+\mu S_{t}e^{r(T-t)}dt+\sigma S_{t}e^{r(T-t)}dw_{t}$$

**Step 4: Group Terms to Find the Final SDE**ô Recall thatô $F_t = S_t e^{r(T-t)}$. Substitutingô $F_t$ô back into the expanded equation yields:

$$df(t,S_{t})=-rF_{t}dt+\mu F_{t}dt+\sigma F_{t}dw_{t}$$

By grouping theô $dt$ô terms together, we arrive at the final SDE for the forward contract:

$$dF_{t}=(\mu-r)F_{t}dt+\sigma F_{t}dw_{t}$$

### 4. Example 2: SDE of an Inverse Contract

**The Problem:**ô Given that the share follows a GBM, find the SDE of the following payoff function:

$$Y_{t}=\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}}$$

**Step 1: Calculate the Partial Derivatives**

- **With respect to time ($t$):**

    $$\frac{\partial Y}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{S_t} \cdot (-re^{r(T-t)}) = -r\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}}$$
    
- **With respect to the underlying asset ($S_t$):**ô Rewriting the function asô $e^{r(T-t)} \cdot S_t^{-1}$, the first derivative via the power rule is:

    $$\frac{\partial Y}{\partial S_{t}} = e^{r(T-t)}(-1 S_{t}^{-2}) = -\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}^{2}}$$
    
- **Second derivative with respect to ($S_t$):**ô Applying the power rule again onô $S_t^{-2}$:

    $$\frac{\partial^{2}Y}{\partial S_{t}^{2}} = -e^{r(T-t)}(-2 S_{t}^{-3}) = \frac{2e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}^{3}}$$


**Step 2: Substitute into ItûÇ's Lemma**ô Plugging these derivatives into the core formula gives:

$$dY(t,S_{t})=-r\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}}dt-\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}^{2}}dS_{t}+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}^{3}}\right)(dS_{t})^{2}$$

**Step 3: Expand using the Multiplication Rules**ô As proven in Section 2,ô $(dS_{t})^{2}=\sigma^{2}S_{t}^{2}dt$.ô Substitute this and the GBM equation forô $dS_t$ô into the expansion:

$$dY(t,S_{t})=-r\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}}dt-\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}^{2}}\{\mu S_{t}dt+\sigma S_{t}dw_{t}\}+\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}^{3}}(\sigma^{2}S_{t}^{2}dt)$$

**Step 4: Simplify and Group Terms**ô Recall thatô $Y_{t}=\frac{e^{r(T-t)}}{S_{t}}$.ô Replacing the expanded terms withô $Y_t$:

$$dY(t,S_{t})=-rY_{t}dt-\mu Y_{t}dt-\sigma Y_{t}dw_{t}+\sigma^{2}Y_{t}dt$$

Finally, grouping all the terms associated withô $dt$ô together gives the completed SDE:

$$dY(t,S_{t})=(\sigma^{2}-r-\mu)Y_{t}dt-\sigma Y_{t}dw_{t}$$
