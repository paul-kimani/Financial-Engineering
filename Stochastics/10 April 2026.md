# Introduction to Stochastic Processes

### Definitions

- **Definition 1:** A stochastic process is a collection of random variables indexed by time, representing systems that evolve under uncertainty.
     
- **Definition 2:** Denoted as $\{X_t\}$ for $t \ge 0$, it models the evolution of a system that exhibits inherent randomness. Unlike deterministic models, you cannot predict the exact future state, only the _probability_ of future states.
    

---

# Ordinary vs. Stochastic Calculus

To understand stochastic calculus, you have to contrast it with the ordinary calculus you use for deterministic functions.

### Ordinary Calculus (Deterministic)

In ordinary calculus, we deal with deterministic functions—systems with no randomness. Their evolution is modeled using **Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)**.

- **The Derivative:** For a function $f(x)$, its derivative is defined by the limit:
    
    $$\frac{df}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x}$$
    
- **The ODE:** An ODE uses $d$ to denote the total derivative and has _only one term_ (the deterministic flow).
    
    $$dX_t = a(X_t, t) dt$$
    

### Stochastic Calculus (Randomness Introduced)

Stochastic calculus extends ordinary calculus to handle systems with random elements. Because financial markets are noisy and unpredictable, we replace ODEs with **Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs)**.

- **The SDE:** An SDE introduces an additional "noise" term.
    
    $$dX_t = \underbrace{a(X_t, t) dt}_{\text{Deterministic/Drift Term}} + \underbrace{b(X_t, t) dW_t}_{\text{Randomness/Volatility Term}}$$
    

---

# The Wiener Process & Brownian Motion

The $W_t$ in the SDE above represents the **Wiener Process**, which is the mathematical foundation of standard Brownian motion. This is the engine that drives the randomness in your models.

### Characteristics of a Wiener Process ($W_t$)

1. **Starts at Zero:** $W_0 = 0$.
    
2. **Independent Increments:** Future movements do not depend on past movements (reflecting the Efficient Market Hypothesis).
    
3. **Normally Distributed Increments:** The change over a time interval $t-s$ is normally distributed with a mean of 0 and a variance equal to the time step: $W_t - W_s \sim N(0, t-s)$.
    
4. **Continuous Path:** The path is continuous everywhere, but it is _differentiable nowhere_ (it is infinitely jagged).
    
5. **No Drift:** Pure standard Brownian motion has no upward or downward trend.
    

### Brownian Motion with Drift

Recall the equation for a generalized Brownian motion:

$$B_t = \underbrace{\mu t}_{\text{Drift}} + \underbrace{\sigma W_t}_{\text{Volatility}}$$

In quantitative trading, $\mu$ represents the expected return of an asset over time, and $\sigma$ represents the market volatility scaling the random shocks ($W_t$).

# Stochastic Integrals and Itô's Lemma

Because a Wiener process is "infinitely jagged" (not differentiable in the standard sense), classical Riemann integration fails.

### The Itô Integral

To integrate functions concerning Brownian motion, stochastic calculus uses the **Itô Integral**. Given a stochastic process $X_t$, it is defined as:

$$\int_0^t X_s dW_s$$

### Itô's Lemma (The Stochastic Chain Rule)

This is the single most important theorem in financial mathematics. **Itô's Lemma is the stochastic counterpart to the chain rule in ordinary calculus.**

If you have a process $X_t$ that follows the general SDE:

$$dX_t = \mu(X_t, t) dt + \sigma(X_t, t) dW_t$$

And you want to find the differential of a new function $f(X_t, t)$—for example, pricing a derivative $f$ based on an underlying asset $X_t$—the solution is given by:

$$df(X_t, t) = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \mu \frac{\partial f}{\partial X_t} + \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X_t^2} \right) dt + \sigma \frac{\partial f}{\partial X_t} dW_t$$

**Why is this different from normal calculus?**

Notice the $\frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial X_t^2}$ term. This is called the **Itô Correction term**. In ordinary calculus, second-order terms approach zero and are ignored. However, in stochastic calculus, the squared differential of a Wiener process is equal to time: $(dW_t)^2 = dt$. Because variance accumulates linearly with time, this second derivative term _must_ be included to accurately model the system.
